I moved to Ho Chi Minh City in 2019 with two suitcases, a tourist visa, and absolutely no idea what I was doing. Five years later, I'm still here. The city got me, the way it gets most people — and now I get to tell you what I wish someone had told me before I landed at Tan Son Nhat at midnight and got into a xe om that took me entirely the wrong direction for twenty minutes.

This guide is for serious movers. If you're considering making HCMC your home base — whether for a year or a decade — here's the real, unsanitized version of what you need to know.

## The Visa Situation

This is where everyone starts and where most people get confused. Let's be direct: Vietnam's visa situation for long-term expats is genuinely complicated and has changed significantly in 2023-2024.

**E-visa (90 days, multiple entry):** Vietnam introduced an extended 90-day e-visa in mid-2023, which has been a game-changer. You apply online, it costs about $25, and it covers most nationalities. Crucially, it's now multiple entry, which means you don't have to do the old "visa run to Cambodia" routine just to reset your stamp. This is the standard entry point for most new arrivals.

**Business Visa (DN):** If you're working for a company, they should sponsor a DN visa for you, which can be extended for 1-2 years. This is the most stable long-term option and what most employed expats are on.

**Work Permit:** If you're employed in Vietnam, you technically need a work permit — a separate document from the visa. Many expats work without one, but it's a legal grey zone you should be aware of. The process involves a criminal background check from your home country and notarized qualifications.

**Investor Visa (DT):** For those setting up their own business. Requires proof of capital investment. Talk to a lawyer before going down this path.

The reality: many long-term expats in HCMC cycle between 90-day e-visas. It's not ideal, but it works. For true long-term stability, a work permit and business visa sponsored by an employer is the way to go.

## Best Neighborhoods for Expats

Where you live in HCMC will define your experience more than almost anything else. Here's the honest breakdown of the main expat areas.

**District 2 / Thao Dien (Thu Duc City)**

The expat heartland. Thao Dien is a leafy, relatively quiet enclave along the Saigon River that has developed into a comfortable, international neighborhood over the past 15 years. It's where you'll find international schools (BIS, Saigon South International, RMIT), Western-style supermarkets, coffee shops that take an hour to make your flat white, and expat families in abundance.

The upside: it's green, relatively calm, and has excellent infrastructure for families. The downside: it's a bubble. You can spend months in Thao Dien and feel like you've barely touched real Saigon. Rents are significantly higher than elsewhere — expect to pay $1,200–$2,500/month for a decent two-bedroom apartment.

**District 1**

The center of everything. Living in District 1 puts you within walking distance of the best restaurants, bars, rooftop spots, and city landmarks. It's loud, it's dense, it's alive at all hours. Ideal for younger expats who want to be in the thick of it and don't need a yard or silence.

Rents vary wildly — from $600/month for a small serviced apartment in a local building to $2,500+ for a modern condo in a development like Vinhomes Central Park or Saigon Royal.

**Binh Thanh District**

The up-and-coming middle ground. Binh Thanh has been developing rapidly and now offers a solid mix of local life and expat-friendly amenities. It's cheaper than D1 or Thao Dien, well-connected, and increasingly popular with younger expats who want authentic Saigon living without paying the D1 premium. Areas around Nguyen Huu Canh and Dien Bien Phu are particularly popular.

## Cost of Living Breakdown

Here's what you can realistically expect to spend per month as a single expat in HCMC (USD approximate, based on 2024 exchange rates around 25,000 VND to $1):

- **Rent (1BR, central):** $600–$1,200
- **Food (mix of local + Western):** $200–$500
- **Transport (Grab + occasional taxi):** $50–$150
- **Utilities (electric, water, internet):** $50–$120
- **Entertainment/nightlife:** $100–$400
- **Gym:** $20–$60
- **Health insurance:** $80–$200

**Total comfortable single expat budget: $1,200–$2,500/month.** This is significantly less than any Western city and provides a genuinely comfortable lifestyle. Live locally, eat local food, use Grab for transport, and you can live very well on $1,200/month. Want Western comforts — air-conditioned gym, imported cheese, cocktails at Chill Sky Bar three nights a week — budget $2,000–$2,500.

## Getting Around

**Grab:** The answer to almost every transport question in HCMC. Grab (Southeast Asia's Uber equivalent) covers cars and motorbike taxis (GrabBike), and works reliably across the city. GrabBike is cheap (40,000–80,000 VND for most urban trips) and the fastest option in traffic. GrabCar is more comfortable but slower in peak hours.

**Motorbike:** The full Saigon experience. Most long-term expats eventually get a motorbike — either an automatic Honda Wave (250,000–400,000 VND/month to rent) or a more characterful older semi-automatic bike. Traffic is genuinely chaotic and there's a real accident rate, but so is everyone's mother's concern when you tell them you're riding a motorbike in Vietnam. Go slow at first, learn the traffic flow, never assume anyone will stop for you.

**Metro Line 1:** Finally open as of late 2024 after an infamous 17-year construction saga. Runs from Ben Thanh (D1) to Suoi Tien (on the outskirts). Useful for getting to Thao Dien and beyond. More on this in our dedicated guide.

**Taxi:** Be aware of scam taxis at the airport. Use Grab from arrivals or find the official Vinasun/Mai Linh taxi stand.

## Tips Nobody Tells You

**Everything takes longer than you think.** Want to open a bank account? Three visits and a notarized passport copy. Registering your address? Depends on your landlord's cooperation. Getting a sim card? Actually easy — done in five minutes at any phone shop.

**Learn a few Vietnamese words.** "Xin chao" (hello), "cam on" (thank you), "bao nhieu?" (how much?), and "khong cam on" (no thank you, for persistent vendors) will take you far. People genuinely appreciate the effort.

**The heat is real.** HCMC has two seasons: hot and wet, and hot and dry. It never gets cold. You will sweat more than you thought possible. Air conditioning is essential. You will eventually stop noticing.

**Healthcare is better than you expect.** FV Hospital, Vinmec, and Columbia Asia are excellent private hospitals with English-speaking staff. Medical costs are a fraction of Western prices. Get health insurance anyway.

**The city will exhaust you and exhilarate you simultaneously.** HCMC is relentless — traffic, heat, noise, construction, opportunity, chaos, warmth, incredible food everywhere you look. Give yourself three months before you decide whether you love it or hate it. Most people land on love.

Welcome to Saigon.
