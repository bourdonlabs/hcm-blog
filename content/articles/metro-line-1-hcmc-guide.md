It has been, not to put too fine a point on it, a very long time coming. HCMC Metro Line 1 — the 19.7km line connecting Ben Thanh station in the city center to Suoi Tien terminal in the Thu Duc area — officially opened to the public on December 22, 2024, after a construction saga that began in 2007 and involved cost blowouts, endless delays, and enough skepticism to fill the entire tunnel three times over.

I rode it on opening weekend. Here's the honest report.

## The Route Breakdown

Metro Line 1 runs from Ben Thanh Station (District 1) northeast through the city, eventually emerging from underground tunnels near Ba Son station and running elevated the rest of the way to Suoi Tien station, which sits next to the Suoi Tien theme park in what is now Thu Duc City.

The 14 stations in order:
1. Ben Thanh (District 1, underground) — the main hub
2. Opera House (District 1, underground)
3. Ba Son (District 1/Binh Thanh border, underground)
4. Van Thanh Park (Binh Thanh, elevated)
5. Tan Cang (Binh Thanh, elevated)
6. Thao Dien (Thu Duc, elevated)
7. An Phu (Thu Duc, elevated)
8. Rach Chiec (Thu Duc, elevated)
9. Phuoc Long (Thu Duc, elevated)
10. Binh Thai (Thu Duc, elevated)
11. Thu Duc (Thu Duc, elevated)
12. High-Tech Park (Thu Duc, elevated)
13. VNU-HCM (Thu Duc, elevated)
14. Suoi Tien (Thu Duc, terminal)

The total journey from Ben Thanh to Suoi Tien takes approximately 30 minutes. That's remarkable — the same trip by road during peak hours can take 60–90 minutes.

The most useful stops for most city residents and expats: Ben Thanh (obvious), Ba Son (access to the emerging arts district), Thao Dien (the main expat residential neighborhood), and An Phu (for the An Phu commercial area).

## How To Buy a Ticket

This was initially the most confusing part and it's now mostly sorted out. You have three options:

**Single journey ticket:** Available at ticket machines at every station. They accept cash (VND) and take about 30 seconds to figure out if you've ever used a transit machine before. The interface is in Vietnamese and English. Fares start at 6,000 VND for the shortest trips and max out at 20,000 VND for the full Ben Thanh to Suoi Tien journey. Dirt cheap.

**Daily/multi-journey cards:** Available at the service counters inside stations. A day pass is 40,000 VND and gives you unlimited rides for that calendar day. Worth it if you're doing more than three journeys.

**HCMC Metro Card (Thẻ tháng):** The monthly card that most regular users are getting. 150,000–300,000 VND depending on the zones you need. Apply at the service counter with ID.

**Mobile payment:** The Metro app (Metro HCMC on iOS and Android) allows QR code ticketing. It works, though the first-time setup requires more patience than it should.

## What the Trains Are Actually Like

The honest answer: excellent. Whatever you think about the delays and the cost overruns, the trains themselves are modern, clean, air-conditioned, and significantly more comfortable than you'd expect. They were built by Hitachi (Japan) and manufactured to a high standard.

The carriages are wide, with plenty of standing room and adequate seating. There are priority seats clearly marked. The stations are well-lit, clean, and have working escalators and elevators. Signage is in Vietnamese and English. The platform screen doors work. Announcements are clear.

First impressions: this is a properly functional metro system, not a liability waiting to happen. The cynics — and there were many, including me at times — owe the project's engineers a modest apology.

Peak hour crowding exists, particularly at Ben Thanh and Thao Dien stations. Expect to wait for a second train during the 7:30–9am and 5–7pm windows. Outside peak hours, finding a seat is not a problem.

## Is It Faster Than Grab?

For the routes it covers: yes, almost always. The Ben Thanh to Thao Dien journey takes 12 minutes on the metro. The same trip by GrabCar during peak hours is routinely 35–50 minutes. GrabBike can sometimes keep up — a skilled rider who knows the back routes — but it's close.

For end-to-end journeys that match a metro station, the metro wins on time. The caveat is the "last mile" problem: the metro drops you at the station, but your actual destination might be a 10-minute walk or Grab ride away. For those of us living exactly on the metro corridor, this is a minor issue. For those in side streets and alleyways, you're still going to need Grab for the final stretch.

The real competitor is GrabBike on short journeys within D1 or Binh Thanh. For trips under 5km without a direct metro connection, the bike still wins on flexibility.

## What It Means For the City

This is the genuinely exciting part. Metro Line 1 is not just a transit system — it's a catalyst for development along its entire corridor. Property prices in Thao Dien and An Phu have already risen in anticipation of the improved connectivity. The Thu Duc City corridor, long underdeveloped relative to the city center, now has a direct, fast link to D1.

The long-term vision includes multiple metro lines — Line 2, Line 3, and several more — that would eventually give HCMC a proper city-wide transit network. These are still years away, but Line 1 is proof the concept works and, crucially, that the city can actually build and operate a functioning metro.

For expats: Thao Dien just got significantly more accessible. If you've been avoiding living there because of the commute, the calculation has changed. The ride from the Thao Dien station into the city center is now genuinely faster than most alternatives.

For the city: this is the beginning of something. HCMC has spent decades being defined by its traffic chaos. Metro Line 1 is the first serious structural answer to that problem. It's not enough yet, but it is something real.

Welcome to the future, Saigon. It only took 17 years.
