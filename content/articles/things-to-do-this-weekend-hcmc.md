Bored in Saigon? Impossible. But we know the feeling of staring at your phone on a Saturday morning, last night's banh mi wrapper still on the coffee table, wondering what to do with the most energetic, overwhelming, ridiculously over-stacked-with-options city in Southeast Asia.

We've done the work for you. Here are 13 things worth doing in Ho Chi Minh City this weekend — from the meditative to the manic.

## 1. Hit Ben Thanh Market Before 7am

Everyone tells you to go to Ben Thanh Market. Almost nobody tells you to go at dawn, when the fruit and vegetable vendors are setting up, the light is extraordinary, and the tourist crowds haven't arrived yet. The early morning version of Ben Thanh is a completely different animal — locals, wholesale buyers, coffee stalls opening, and the genuine daily pulse of the city. Show up around 5:30–6am, have a ca phe sua da from the nearest street cart, and watch the city wake up around you.

## 2. Walk the Full Length of Dong Khoi Street

Dong Khoi is Saigon's grand boulevard — the one that connects the Cathedral to the river — and it rewards slow, attentive walking. Look up at the colonial architecture, duck into the art galleries (there are more than a dozen on this stretch), browse the lacquerware shops, and end at the Majestic Hotel for a coffee on the terrace overlooking the Saigon River. Total walk time: 45 minutes, or 3 hours if you stop everywhere you should.

## 3. Take a Motorbike Tour of the Backstreets

Book a motorbike tour (Saigon Street Eats, Vespa Adventures, and XO Tours are all excellent) and let someone local show you the Saigon that exists in the alleyways and hems between the boulevards. These tours typically run 3–4 hours, stop at 6–8 street food spots, and take you through neighborhoods most visitors never see. Life-changing is a strong phrase. We're using it anyway.

## 4. Visit the War Remnants Museum

Not a fun activity. An essential one. The War Remnants Museum on Vo Van Tan Street is one of the most powerful museum experiences in Southeast Asia — a unflinching documentation of the American War from the Vietnamese perspective. Block out two hours, go slowly, and don't skip the third floor. The photography exhibitions are extraordinary. It will stay with you.

## 5. Take the Saigon River Sunset Cruise

Multiple operators run 1.5–2 hour sunset cruises on the Saigon River, departing from the Bach Dang Wharf area. The city looks completely different from the water — you see the skyline, the industrial waterfront, the bridges, and the quieter reaches of the river all at once. Most include dinner or drinks. Go for the sundowner-only option if you want the view without the set menu.

## 6. Explore the Art Galleries of District 3

District 3 has quietly become HCMC's most interesting arts neighborhood. The stretch around Nguyen Thi Minh Khai, Tran Quy Cap, and Mac Dinh Chi streets is dotted with independent galleries, artist studios, and concept spaces showing contemporary Vietnamese art alongside international names. The Mai Gallery, Manzi Art Space (technically District 3/Ba Dinh border), and Craig Thomas Gallery are all worth checking. Most are free to enter.

## 7. Eat Your Way Through Cho Ben Thanh's Food Court... No Wait, Go to Cho Cu Instead

Avoid the tourist-priced stalls at Ben Thanh and head instead to Cho Cu (Old Market) on Ton That Dam Street — a covered market beloved by locals for fresh produce, street snacks, and a genuinely alive market atmosphere. This is where Saigonese grandmothers buy their vegetables and where you'll find banh cuon, com tam, and bun bo Hue that hasn't been adjusted for Western palates.

## 8. Rent a Bicycle and Cross the Bridge to Thanh Da Peninsula

Thanh Da is a low-key river island about 20 minutes from District 1 by bicycle, accessible via the Thu Duc Bridge. Once you cross, the city effectively disappears — there are rice paddies, quiet roads, riverside cafes, and almost no tourists. It's the closest thing to escaping Saigon without leaving it. Rent a bicycle from any of the hire shops near the Binh Thanh riverside.

## 9. Have a Cocktail at a Heritage Bar

Saigon Saigon at the Caravelle, the Long Bar at the Majestic, or the rooftop at the Rex Hotel — the city's colonial-era hotels have bars that carry genuine history. Journalists, diplomats, and CIA operatives drank in these rooms during the war. Order an overpriced Saigon Sling, sit in the rattan chair, and spend five minutes really thinking about the weight of the city around you.

## 10. Take a Cooking Class

There are excellent half-day cooking classes run in HCMC that include a market visit to buy ingredients and then instruction on making 3–5 Vietnamese dishes. Saigon Cooking Class at Hoa Tuc (in a former opium factory, now a beautiful restaurant) is our favorite. You'll go home knowing how to make pho, fresh spring rolls, and caramelized clay-pot fish. Worth every dong.

## 11. Stroll Nguyen Hue Walking Street on a Weekend Evening

On Friday and Saturday evenings, the broad pedestrian boulevard of Nguyen Hue — which runs from the City Hall down toward the river — fills with families, couples, street performers, food vendors, and an enormous informal party atmosphere. It's touristy, yes, but also genuinely vibrant and a perfect snapshot of contemporary Saigon's energy.

## 12. Discover the Underground Coffee Scene in District 4

District 4 is Saigon's best-kept secret. A five-minute bridge ride from D1 but light-years away in character, D4 is where you'll find multi-story concept cafes built into old warehouses, young artists' studios above seafood restaurants, and an increasingly interesting independent coffee scene. Start at Cong Caphe on Hoang Dieu Street and ask the locals where to go next.

## 13. End It All on Bui Vien — If You Dare

The walking street of Bui Vien in the backpacker district is controversial. Is it tacky? Yes. Is the music too loud? Absolutely. Are the Tiger beers served at 3am a terrible idea? Categorically. Is it also an experience totally unique to Saigon, almost hallucinogenically energetic, and in its own chaotic way kind of magnificent?

Also yes.

Save it for last. Go with no expectations. Lose something and find something else.

Saigon keeps its promise.
